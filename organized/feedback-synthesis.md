# Customer Feedback Synthesis (Jan–Apr)

*Synthesized from 4 months of feedback in `inherited-chaos/customer-feedback/`*

## The Headline

**"Fine but forgettable."** This exact phrase appears in **three separate months** of feedback. It is, in the previous manager's own words, "our problem in three words." People don't dislike Basecamp Rewards — they don't *think about it at all*.

## The Trend Is Going the Wrong Way

| Metric | Jan | Feb | Mar | Apr |
|--------|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| Very Satisfied | 12% | 10% | 8% | 7% |
| Neutral | 41% | 44% | 47% | 48% |
| Dissatisfied (any) | 13% | 15% | 16% | 18% |

Satisfaction is slowly eroding and the "neutral" middle is *growing* every month — apathy is winning.

**The killer stat (April):**
- NPS for **Basecamp overall: 67** (strong — people love the brand)
- NPS for **Basecamp Rewards: 12** (weak)

That gap is the whole story.

## Recurring Themes

### 1. Forgettable / invisible
- "I forget about it mostly" · "Honestly forgot I was a member until you emailed me" · "I have the app somewhere on my phone" · "basecamp coffee has a rewards program? news to me"

### 2. No personality — doesn't feel like Basecamp
- "This program has no personality. It's just points." · "It feels like corporate stuff." · "Generic. Could be any coffee chain." · "I don't feel like a valued customer, I feel like a transaction."

### 3. Tiers are meaningless
- "What's an Explorer? Am I supposed to care?" · "I've been coming here a year and I'm still a Trailblazer. What does that even mean?" · Baristas can't explain them either.

### 4. Points feel slow & joyless
- "Takes forever to earn a free drink" · "What's the point of points if there's nothing fun about it?"

### 5. Losing to competitors on *experience*, not coffee
- "I go to Starbucks for rewards, here for good coffee." · "Starbucks at least has games. Dutch Bros remembers my name."

## The Hidden Opportunity (customers are literally asking for it)

Buried in the in-store comments, customers keep gesturing at the same unmet need — **personalization around their coffee tastes**:

- "I wish you could help me find new drinks to try. I always get the same thing."
- "What if you helped people find their signature drink?"
- Barista: *"People light up when we ask about their coffee preferences, but the app doesn't capture any of that."*

And the comment that ties it all together:
> **"Your coffee has personality. Your rewards program doesn't. Fix that and you'd have something."**

## The Real Problem (in one sentence)

The program is a generic transactional points system bolted onto a brand that people love *because it feels personal* — so it feels like "corporate stuff," not like Basecamp.
