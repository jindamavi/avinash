# Synthesis Feedback — Three Perspectives

*Three sub-agents reviewed `analysis/comprehensive-research-synthesis.md` from their own perspectives. Here's what each one said.*

---

## (ಠ_ಠ) Exec — Leadership / ROI

The core problem is a leaky bucket: signups up 2.2x (1,850 → 4,120) while monthly actives went the wrong way (1,200 → 480), frequency down 3.8 → 1.6, and 90-day retention at 14%. We've sunk $40K and program NPS cratered from 34 to 12. The points mechanic isn't moving the needle.

The competitive read is sound — Starbucks pulls ~60% retention off personalization; points-only Peet's is "just there." If identity-based personalization moves our 14% toward even 25–30%, that's a real revenue story we can model. The "discovery-as-identity" whitespace is corroborated across every month of feedback — that's data convergence, not a hunch.

**The pushback:** the synthesis jumps from diagnosis straight to "coffee personality quiz" with zero numbers attached. What's the projected retention lift? Build cost on top of the $40K? 90-day success milestone and kill criterion? "Replace points with belonging" can't go to the board — belonging doesn't show up on a P&L, repeat visits do.

**Recommendation:** Greenlight a tightly-scoped, low-cost pilot with a one-page business case — target lift, cost ceiling, 90-day milestone, defined off-ramp, and a control group for clean attribution. The diagnosis earns a test, not a blank check.

---

## (◠‿◠) Product Designer — UX / Experience

The core problem isn't retention mechanics — it's that the program has no soul. Brand NPS (66) vs. program NPS (12) IS the diagnosis. We built a points tracker and called it a relationship. Members aren't leaving angry, they're leaving the way you leave a waiting room — nothing held them because there was nothing to hold. "Treated like a transaction" every month isn't a feature request, it's a feelings report.

Competitors win on the human/identity layer. Dutch Bros remembers your name and you feel SEEN; our app shows a number going up. One becomes a story you tell friends, the other becomes background noise by week two.

**The energy:** "Coffee personality" answers the question members couldn't articulate — "what kind of coffee person am I?" That's an identity question, not a discount question. "Espresso Explorer" can be a badge they broadcast, a lens that makes every rec feel personally chosen, and a reason to reopen the app that has nothing to do with points. The magic word is *me* — this app knows me.

**The caution:** A static label is just a tracker with a cuter name, and indifference comes right back. The identity has to evolve, surprise them, get reflected back by the humans at the counter. If they can't feel something in the first 30 seconds — and a little more known each week — we've built another waiting room. The bar isn't "personalized." It's "they'd be a little sad to lose it."

---

## (•‿•) Barista Lead — Ground-Level Operations

The synthesis nailed it: people aren't mad, they just forget we exist. Nobody walks up and asks how many points they have. The program is invisible. What I DO hear every shift: "what should I try?", "what's good today?", "I always get the same thing, surprise me." That's the gold.

**The worry on the quiz:** the instinct is right, but does it feel like Basecamp or like corporate trying to be fun? "Espresso Explorer" badges can go cheesy fast and regulars smell it. But they'd light up if I handed them a drink and said "you're a natural-process person, try this Ethiopian." Identity without a real drink in the cup is just a horoscope.

**The operations risk:** if this lives only in the confusing app, it's dead — customers already ask US to navigate the app for them. Baristas have to be able to explain it in one sentence at the register and act on it. Give me a screen that says "this regular likes bright, fruity, light roast" and I'll make magic.

**The vote:** Lean hard into discovery-as-identity, but anchor it to the human moment, not the app. The quiz is the hook to capture preferences we have zero of today. The payoff has to be a recommendation — a barista who knows your taste, a "we picked this for you." Nail that and regulars would love it.

---

## Where They Converge

All three, from totally different angles, point at the same thing:

- **Exec:** the ROI lever is personalization, not points.
- **Designer:** give the program a soul through identity, not transactions.
- **Barista Lead:** customers already ask "what should I try?" — help them discover their coffee identity.

Three perspectives. One direction: **a coffee personality experience.**
